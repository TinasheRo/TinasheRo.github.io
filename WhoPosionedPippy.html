Who Poisoned Pippy?

An interactive mystery experience where players investigate clues, interpret evidence, and uncover the truth behind Pippy's poisoning.

Role

Interface Designer & Product Management support

Duration

14 weeks

Tools

Balsamiq | Figma | HTML | CSS | JS | Github

Final website: Who Poisoned Pippy?

01 - The Opportunity

How might we create a mystery experience that feels engaging, and Accessible while encouraging active investigation

Our challenge was to design an experience where players become investigators, piecing together evidence across multiple perspectives before reaching their own conclusion

The project aim:

  • Narrative immersion
  • Meaningful player agency
  • Accessible interaction design
  • Clear evidence discovery

02 - Contribution

My focus:

  • information architecture
  • Wireframe and interface design
  • Design system creation in Figma
  • Documentation and project management support
  • Sprint planning and team coordination

03 - Process & Collaboration

Running a four person team like a small agile studio

As documentation lead, I worked alongside 4 peers to keep a 14-week project spanning visual art, writing, interface design and front-end development moving in parallel rather than stalling on a single critical path. We split the work into five sprints, letting design, scripting and development progress together instead of waiting on each other:

  • Sprint 1 — Planning & Concept (Weeks 4–5): evaluated five project concepts as a team, voted to combine a murder mystery with a comic-style story adaptation, and defined the cast and their relationships.
  • Sprint 2 — Skeleton (Weeks 6–7): roles were formalised and I worked with a peer on paper prototypes and low-fidelity wireframes in Balsamiq, which we then carried into high-fidelity Figma prototypes covering the introduction page, perspective selection and case file.
  • Sprint 3 — Narrative & Comic Production (Easter–Week 8): each of us scripted assigned chapters across both perspectives, covering scene description, dialogue and panel sequence.
  • Sprint 4 — Integration (Weeks 9–11): the homepage, chapter layouts and perspective pages were built out in HTML/CSS/JS.
  • Sprint 5 — Testing & Submission (Weeks 12–14): artwork, interface and development came together into a working build; I compiled the copyright appendix ahead of the 27 May deadline.

From Sprint 2 onwards, I maintained the team's shared task tracker in Google Sheets. Every task was assigned to a named team member, given a priority level and a weekly status — not started, in progress, completed or blocked — so anyone could see where the project stood at a glance and step in early if something was stuck.

The task tracker I maintained to allocate and monitor workload across all five sprints

Alongside the tracker, I recorded the minutes for every weekly meeting, capturing decisions and action items so that choices made informally — over WhatsApp, in sub-meetings, or in the room — had one place the whole team could check back against.

Reflection: working within developer capabilities

A layout that looks correct in Figma doesn't automatically behave the same way once it's built on a framework like Bootstrap. We ran into this directly when Bootstrap's utility classes overrode the custom clamp() sizing on the back button, and again during testing when the case file wouldn't centre correctly in HTML/CSS. Adopting a 12-column grid early and carrying it consistently from wireframe, to Figma, to CSS reduced a lot of this friction — but it also meant accepting that some interface decisions had to be made with the developers' constraints in mind from the start, rather than handed over as a finished design afterwards. If I ran this process again, I'd bring development into the wireframing stage even earlier, so technical limits are part of the conversation from day one instead of something we discover during integration.

04 - Design System

Defining the visual language before a single line of code was written

Before development began, I worked with the team to define the project's design system in Figma — colour, typography and core UI components — so that whoever was building or designing a page was working from the same reference rather than improvising individually.

We built the colour palette around four functional roles: a near-black background and dark panel colour for the noir tone fitting a murder mystery, off-white primary text for legibility without harsh contrast, and a single deep red accent (#C1121F) reserved for interactive elements, highlighted names and clues — teaching players to read red as "interactive" without needing instructions.

Colour palette and typography hierarchy I helped define in Figma

Typography followed the same logic: a three-level scale using Patrick Hand for headings to reinforce the handwritten comic aesthetic, and Valera Round for body text to keep longer passages legible. Base sizes were set in Figma as fixed pixel values, then translated into fluid clamp() values in CSS, preserving the proportional relationship between heading, subheading and body text across every screen size.

05 - Reflection

Designing for accessibility and feasibility, not just aesthetics

The biggest lesson from this whole project came down to one feature: an animated gradient background that shifted as you scrolled, meant to make the comic panels feel moodier and more noir. We'd had it in mind from the start. It was on the moodboard, it was in the early Figma prototypes but by the time we actually got into development, it just didn't hold up..

Part of it was that it was way harder to build than any of us expected. It kept showing up as a blocker in our scrum meetings, and it took a few sessions before it was even working properly across the pages. Something that looked like a nice little visual touch in Figma ended up eating a real chunk of dev time, and honestly, none of us had thought to flag that risk before we'd already locked the idea in.

The other problem was accessibility. When we actually tested it against WCAG 2.1, the gradient was dragging contrast down between the background and the comic text, and in some of the transition areas it dipped below what Success Criterion 1.4.5 asks for. The fact that it never stopped moving was also an issue under 2.2.2, which says moving content needs to be pausable. Once we'd hit both of those, too hard to build properly and not accessible, there wasn't really a case for keeping it. We dropped the animation and kept a static, scroll triggered version instead, which still gave us the mood shift without dragging contrast.

Looking back, the gradient wasn't really a one-off, it was the same story as the back button clashing with Bootstrap, and the case file not centring properly. Each time, something that looked done on paper turned into rework the moment it met actual code. If I did this again, I'd want developers in the room earlier, while we're still sketching ideas, not after they've already been designed and signed off on. Agree on a fallback before it's a problem, and actually say out loud when we're compromising, so swapping the animated gradient for a static one reads as a decision we made together, not as the design just losing.


Guerrilla testing at the Interactive Media showcase

Who Poisoned Pippy? was exhibited at the Interactive Media showcase, running on a public display for visitors to pick up and play without any instruction from us. Watching people interact with it in that setting turned into a kind of guerrilla testing but informal, unplanned usability observation on real visitors rather than recruited participants in a structured session.

The clearest pattern I noticed was that several players stalled on a section of the story that required them to scroll down to continue. There was no visual cue, no arrow, no partially cut-off panel, nothing, to suggest there was more content below, so a number of people assumed that screen was the end of the chapter and either stopped or gave up.

It's a small thing, but it's exactly the kind of issue that's invisible in a design file and only shows up once real people are interacting with the build on their own. If I revisited the page, I'd add a clear scroll indicator at any point where continuing the story depends on scrolling rather than clicking, even something as simple as a partially visible next panel or a subtle animated cue would have closed that gap.

"What would have helped: surfacing feasibility in the same meeting as the concept, and watching real people use it sooner."
